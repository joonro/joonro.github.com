Research and Dissertation¶

My main research interest is evaluating the effectiveness of firms’ marketing strategies in settings where dynamic components such as forward-looking consumers, secondary market, and network effects are important. I am fascinated by how these factors significantly alter the effectiveness of marketing strategies, and how failure to take these factors into account can lead to suboptimal, or even erroneous policy implications. Specifically, I apply empirical industrial organization models to marketing; I employ structural models and conduct counterfactual analyses to evaluate the effectiveness of marketing and economic policies.

Co-Chairs: Jason Duan and Leigh McAlister

Committee Members: Ty Henderson, Raghunath S. Rao, Stephen P. Ryan (Economics)

Essay 1: Pricing and Resale Market Strategy for Durable Goods: A Dynamic Equilibrium Model of the Video Game Market¶

I develop a dynamic structural model to quantify the impact of the used game market on market outcomes, incorporating forward-looking consumers, intertemporal price discrimination, and the used goods market. Importantly, I make a significant methodological contribution as my model can recover demand parameters with only price information in the absence of sales data by explicitly solving for the supply side equilibrium. Using a unique dataset that I collected from the web, I found the benefits from prohibiting resale largely depends on the shape of demand distribution, suggesting game specific optimal strategies regarding the used game market.

Essay 2. A Model of Downloadable Contents: Add-on and the Used Goods Market¶

I extend the model in the first essay to examine the implications of an additional marketing strategy, post-release add-ons. Many video game producers release add-ons called downloadable contents (DLC) for a relatively low price, which extend playing time of their video games. This can be an effective strategy to reduce competition from the used goods market. This is because in order to play the DLC, a consumer must physically have the original copy of the game; hence, forward-looking consumers who anticipate that a DLC will be released in the near future will likely to hold on to their disc rather than selling them, effectively reducing the used copy supply. Counterfactual analysis will shed light on profit implications and optimal release-timing of the DLC.
