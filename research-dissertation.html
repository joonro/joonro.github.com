Research and Dissertation¶

My main research interest is evaluating of firms’ marketing strategies in the setting where dynamic components such as forward-looking consumers, secondary market, and network effects are important. I found it fascinating how these factors can significantly alter the effectiveness of the marketing strategies, and failure to take these into account often leads to wrong policy implications.

I study these issues by applying empirical industrial organization models to marketing; I employ structural models and conduct counterfactual analysis to evaluate marketing and economic policies. I enjoy computationally challenging problems and implementing efficient algorithms. I am passionate about reproducible science, and I have released a couple of scientific libraries that I have developed for my projects as open source software packages.

Co-Chairs: Jason Duan and Leigh McAlister

Committee Members: Ty Henderson, Raghunath S. Rao, Stephen P. Ryan (Economics)

Dissertation Overview¶

Essay 1: A Dynamic Equilibrium Model of Durable Goods Market: Intertemporal Pricing and Secondary Market¶

I develop a dynamic structural model of durable goods, incorporating both demand and supply side under the presence of used goods market in the first essay of my dissertation. This model incorporates firm’s optimal intertemporal pricing, forward-looking consumers, and their interaction with the secondary market. By exploiting firm’s optimal policy, this model can recover demand parameters by only using prices, without observing sales information. I apply this model to video games industry, and estimate game-specific demand. With estimated parameters, I evaluate several marketing and economic policies by counterfactual analysis, notably the evaluation of the profit after elimination of the secondary market.

Essay 2. A Model of Downloadable Contents¶

I consider additional marketing strategy of post-release add-on. Many producers in the video game industry releases add-ons called downloadable contents (DLC). The idea of this paper is that this add-on can be an effective strategy to reduce the competition from the used goods market. This is because in order to play the DLC, a consumer must have the original copy of the game. Hence, if consumers expect future release of the add-ons, ones who want to play the DLC will hold on to their disc instead of selling them, effectively reducing the used copy supply. I plan to extend the model of the first essay and add the release of DLC as another control variable. Counterfactual analysis will shed light on the profit implications and optimal release timing of the DLC.
