Research and Dissertation¶

I use empirical models to evaluate the effectiveness of marketing strategies in various settings such as durable goods and entertainment consumption. I am especially interested in dynamic components such as forward-looking consumers, secondary markets, WOM, and network effects. I am fascinated by how these factors significantly alter the effectiveness of marketing strategies, and how failing to take these factors into account can lead to suboptimal, or even erroneous policy implications. I use both individual and aggregate level data to analyze agents’ behavior and make policy recommendations.

Secondly, I am also interested in marketing analytics and modeling consumer behavior. I have ideal quantitative and computational skillsets to be a successful researcher in analytics using big data, and I have ample experience in gathering and processing large amount of data.

Co-Chairs: Jason Duan and Leigh McAlister

Committee Members: Ty Henderson, Raghunath S. Rao, Stephen P. Ryan (Economics)

Essay 1: Pricing and Resale Market Strategy for Durable Goods: A Dynamic Equilibrium Model of Video Games¶

I develop a dynamic structural model to quantify the impact of the used goods market on pricing and profits in the video game industry and study the managerial implications of resale restrictions. My model incorporates forward-looking consumers, intertemporal price discrimination, and the used goods market. To solve the challenge of lacking used goods transaction data, I explicitly incorporate the supply side in the estimation and infer sales volume from the observed prices and equilibrium conditions. Using a unique dataset I collected from the web, I found that the profit gains from prohibiting resale largely depends on the shape of the game’s demand distribution, suggesting game-specific optimal strategies regarding prohibiting the used game market.

Essay 2. A Model of Downloadable Contents: Add-On and the Used Goods Market¶

I extend the model in my first dissertation essay to examine the implications of an additional marketing strategy, i.e., post-release add-ons. Many video game producers release add-ons called downloadable contents (DLC) for a relatively low price, which extend consumers’ video game-playing time. This can be an effective strategy to delay consumers’ used goods resale which would in turn reduce competition from the used goods market. This is because, to play the DLC, a consumer must physically have the original copy of the game; forward-looking consumers who anticipate that DLC will be released in the near future will likely to hold on to their disc rather than selling them, effectively reducing the used copy supply. Counterfactual analysis will shed light on how much profit can be gained with this marketing strategy and the optimal timing of DLC release.

Essay 3. Variety Seeking in Experiential Consumption: Evidence from the Movie Industry¶

Compared to typical consumer packaged goods, a number of experiential product categories such as films, books, video games, and even vacation packages are characterized by single consumption episodes; consumers generally do not consume the same product multiple times, let alone consecutively. When it is natural for consumers to choose a different product for every consumption episode, is there any state dependence, and if so how can we capture it? The current literature lacks a framework that can understand and measure consumer state dependence or variety seeking in these settings. We add to the literature by using an attribute-based approach and creating a genre similarity index to capture state dependence. Measuring state dependence in experiential goods is particularly interesting because of two competing forces at play, making the prediction less clear. The single consumption nature implies greater uncertainty and perceived risk in each consumption decision, and it can promote state dependence; however, prior research suggests consumers want variety for hedonic consumption. Further, we examine the role of online product ratings in alleviating uncertainty associated with less familiar movie genres, and consequently reducing genre state dependence. We use a unique individual-level dataset of theater movie watching, which has information about specific dates and times of consumption. Hence it allows us to observe actual (vs. planned) consumption behavior. We estimate a choice model which incorporates state dependence, persistent consumer preferences, and the impact of movie ratings. We find that consumers choose different genres when watching multiple movies a day; but this variety-seeking behavior is significantly reduced just one day past their previous consumption, meaning consumers tend to watch similar genres repeatedly. Further, we find that positive movie ratings provided by other consumers can reduce this inertia, as the impact of positive ratings are more prominent when the subsequent genre is different from the previously watched one. These findings provide useful insights for managers, especially when making recommendations for experiential products based on consumers’ purchase history.
